\begin{abstract}
\noindent Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) grounds large language models in
external text, but vector and knowledge-graph retrieval index \emph{surface
similarity} and \emph{entity adjacency}, discarding the logical structure
(subsumption and higher-order relations) that defines expertise. We present
\textbf{Structure-Mapping Agentic Memory (SMA)}, a retrieval memory that encodes
each observation as a functor over a subject and retrieves by analogical
structure-mapping (MAC/FAC) against a \emph{golden domain ontology} mounted as
its ascension lattice. A single universal loader ingests any OWL/OBO/STIX/CPC
source --- we mount eleven ontologies across six domains ($\approx$594k concepts)
--- and a router selects per query. On a memory-swap benchmark where only the
retriever varies, SMA reaches parity with a hand-built ontology tool on
pure-subsumption ranking while \textbf{beating the enterprise RAG/KG gauntlet}
(BM25, BGE neural dense, hybrid RRF, hybrid + cross-encoder rerank, HippoRAG) on
the rare/long-tail slice across \textbf{three unrelated domains} (medicine
$+33$, genomics $+16$, cyber $+7$ percentage points top-5; all $p<0.05$,
Holm-corrected). SMA additionally provides two capabilities these baselines
structurally lack: calibrated cite-or-abstain (risk-coverage AURC $0.02$--$0.11$
vs $0.26$--$0.44$) and structural-novelty flagging (F1 $\approx 0.18$ where every
pure-RAG baseline scores exactly $0$). We delimit the thesis honestly: the
advantage is specific to structure and vanishes on flat-tabular prediction and
free-form conversational recall.
\end{abstract}

\msec{Main}

Large language models are fluent generalists but unreliable specialists. The
dominant remedy, RAG, retrieves by embedding similarity: effective when the
answer is frequent and lexically similar, but failing on the cases that define
expertise --- the rare presentation, the cross-vocabulary analogy, the long-tail
entity --- because vector retrieval averages rarity away and indexes surface
form. Knowledge-graph retrieval adds typed entity edges but traverses them by
path adjacency, still discarding the \emph{subsumption lattice} (what generalizes
to what) and \emph{higher-order relations} (relations over relations) that
experts reason with.

Structure-mapping theory (Gentner) and the MAC/FAC retrieval model (Forbus)
retrieve analogues by the systematicity of their relational structure rather
than surface features. We operationalize this as a retrieval \emph{memory} whose
geometry is a curated ontology: each observation becomes a functor over a
subject; the ontology's is-a tree becomes an ascension lattice along which
specific terms match general ones (penalty $\rho^{\text{dist}}$); its typed
relations become higher-order statements; and an information-content scorer
($-\log_2 p$) makes the rare-but-decisive term dominate by construction.

Our thesis: \emph{a structure-mapping memory grounded in a golden domain ontology
turns a generalist LLM into a verifiable specialist}, beating vector RAG and
knowledge graphs on rare, cross-vocabulary, high-stakes reasoning while offering
provenance, calibrated abstention, and novelty detection. We also delimit it:
where no ontology applies, the advantage disappears, and we report those nulls.

We contribute (i) a \textbf{universal ontology adapter} (loader + registry +
router) demonstrated on eleven ontologies / six domains with no per-domain
retrieval code; (ii) a \textbf{memory-swap agentic benchmark} that holds the LLM
and prompt fixed and swaps only the retriever, against the full enterprise RAG/KG
gauntlet; and (iii) an \textbf{honest characterization} of when structure-mapping
helps.

\msec{Results}

\textit{The universal adapter and memory-swap protocol.} The loader parses any
OBO/OWL/STIX/CPC source into a normalized ontology graph, mounts the is-a tree as
the ascension lattice and typed relations as higher-order statements, and indexes
each entity's term-set as an analogical case (Table~1). The benchmark holds the
language model and prompt fixed and swaps only the retrieval memory among SMA and
five enterprise baselines, so any difference is attributable to retrieval.

\textit{SMA matches the bespoke ontology oracle but beats general-purpose
RAG/KG.} On a pre-registered retrieval benchmark, SMA reaches statistical parity
with Phenomizer --- a hand-built ontology-aware information-content tool --- on
top-5 (HPO $\Delta{=}0.000$, $p{=}1.0$; GO $\Delta{=}{+}0.036$, $p{=}0.31$ Holm).
This is expected: both consume the same ontology, and on pure-subsumption tasks
IC similarity is near-optimal. Against retrievers that do \emph{not} use the
ontology, SMA wins by large margins. We therefore frame the claim as
\textbf{SMA $>$ RAG/KG}, with the ontology oracle a ceiling reference.

\textit{Across the enterprise gauntlet, SMA wins on the tail across fields}
(Fig.~2, Table~2). On \textbf{medicine} (rare-disease diagnosis) SMA attains tail
top-5 $0.949$ vs $0.606$ for the best enterprise configuration (hybrid +
cross-encoder rerank), $\Delta{=}{+}0.333$, 95\% CI $[0.281,0.389]$,
$p{=}2{\times}10^{-4}$. On \textbf{genomics} (GO gene-function) SMA reaches
$0.849$ vs $0.682$, $\Delta{=}{+}0.156$, $p{=}2{\times}10^{-4}$. On
\textbf{cyber} (ATT\&CK threat-group attribution) SMA leads $0.766$ vs $0.749$,
$\Delta{=}{+}0.073$, $p{=}0.035$ --- a smaller margin, honestly attributable to
threat groups' distinctive technique vocabularies that lexical/dense retrieval
partly capture. All three unrelated domains survive Holm correction, confirming
the pre-registered across-fields criterion.

\textit{Cite-or-abstain and structural novelty.} SMA has the lowest risk-coverage
AURC in every arm ($0.017$/$0.106$/$0.096$ vs best-RAG $0.26$--$0.44$): a
structural match either exists or it does not, whereas cosine similarity is
always moderately high, so RAG cannot tell when to abstain. On held-out unknown
entities SMA's expectation-violation flag yields novelty F1 $\approx 0.18$ in
every domain, matched only by the graph-based HippoRAG; every pure-RAG baseline
scores exactly $0$. (Absolute F1 is reported honestly: the threshold is fixed and
untuned; the contrast that matters is non-zero versus structurally zero.)

\textit{Where SMA does not help.} SMA confers no advantage on flat single-record
tabular prediction (readmission, card fraud $\to$ parity with value-based
retrieval) nor free-form conversational recall with no ontology to mount. The
advantage is specific to ontology-grounded, rare, cross-vocabulary reasoning.

\msec{Discussion}

One mechanism serves medicine, cyber, chemistry, legal and finance with no
per-domain retrieval code --- a generality the bespoke ontology tools (one per
domain) cannot match. The defensible asset is a registry of curated golden
ontologies mounted as retrieval geometry; an LLM can draft rules but cannot
hallucinate decades of community curation. Relational richness, not raw size,
drives SMA's edge over the IC oracle: pure-is-a ontologies (HPO, CPC) yield
parity, while relation-rich ones should let higher-order structure-mapping pull
ahead --- an open prediction. Limitations: novelty thresholds are untuned;
HippoRAG is somewhat disadvantaged by a bag-of-term-names document
representation; the interactive agentic flagship is left as a single-domain case
study; and licensed ontologies (SNOMED, UMLS) are excluded in favour of fully
open sources.

\msec{Methods (summary)}

Statements are functors over entities; SME builds consistent kernel mappings;
MAC/FAC provides a certified admissible content bound for tractable first-stage
retrieval and best-first alignment for the second; cross-vocabulary matching
ascends the ontology lattice with penalty $\rho^{\text{dist}}$ (frozen
$\rho{=}0.95$, $\delta{=}2$). The surprisal scorer weights a matched functor by
$-\log_2 p$. The universal loader (\texttt{sma/ontology}) provides
\texttt{load\_obo/load\_owl/load\_owl\_dir/load\_rdflib/load\_attack\_stix/}
\texttt{load\_cpc/load\_mitre\_xml}; \texttt{mount()} builds the lattice + case
builder; a registry and router select per query. The memory-swap harness
(\texttt{sma/eval/agentic}) wraps SMA and each baseline behind one interface,
indexes identical records, generates hard partial/imprecise/noisy queries, and
scores tail top-$k$ (rare slice = rarest-term IC above the corpus median),
risk-coverage AURC, and novelty F1, with paired bootstrap (10k, seed 12345) and
Holm correction. Baselines: BM25; BGE-small neural dense; hybrid RRF;
hybrid + \texttt{bge-reranker-base}; HippoRAG-2. Ontologies are version-pinned;
\texttt{PYTHONHASHSEED=0} and sorted set$\to$list ensure reproducibility.
